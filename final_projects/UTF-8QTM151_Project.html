Subsection 1: How Income Levels Relate to Health and GDP

This chart compares two things between countries with different income levels: their average performance on health rights and their average income per person.

  • The left plot shows the average health rights score for each group. This score reflects how well countries are doing at meeting basic health needslike making sure people survive into adulthood, children live past age five, and people have access to medical care.

  • The right plot shows the average GDP per capita (gross domestic product per person), which is a common way of measuring how wealthy a country is.

There are two colored bars for each plot:

  • The teal bars show the average for low and middle income countries
  • The gray bars show the average for high income countries

# Step 1: Access housing scores for high-income and low/mid-income countries using the High_Income_Adjusted and Low_Mid_Income_Adjusted datasets.
# create new datasets'housing_hiy' and 'housing_lmy'.

housing_hiy = High_Income_Adjusted[
    (High_Income_Adjusted['High_Income_Country'] == 1) # Select rows with a 1 for high-income countries in the 'High_Income_Country' column of the High_Income_Adjusted dataset.
][['Country', 'Year', 'HiY_Housing_All_IAScore', 'U5Survival_All_MRGDP']] # Filter the datasets to include only the relevant columns: 'Country', 'Year', 'Housing_Score', and 'GDP_per_capita'.

housing_lmy = Low_Mid_Income_Adjusted[
    (Low_Mid_Income_Adjusted['High_Income_Country'] == 0) # Select rows with a 0 for high-income countries in the 'High_Income_Country' column of the Low_Mid_Income_Adjusted dataset.
][['Country', 'Year', 'LMY_Housing_All_IAScore', 'U5Survival_All_MRGDP']] # Filter the datasets to include only the relevant columns: 'Country', 'Year', 'Housing_Score', and 'GDP_per_capita'.

# Step 2: Rename columns for consistency
housing_hiy = housing_hiy.rename(columns={
    'HiY_Housing_All_IAScore': 'Housing_Score',
    'U5Survival_All_MRGDP': 'GDP_per_capita'
})
housing_lmy = housing_lmy.rename(columns={
    'LMY_Housing_All_IAScore': 'Housing_Score',
    'U5Survival_All_MRGDP': 'GDP_per_capita'
})

# Step 3: Add income group label
housing_hiy['Income_Group'] = 'High Income'
housing_lmy['Income_Group'] = 'Low/Mid Income'

# Step 4: Combine both datasets
housing_df = pd.concat([housing_hiy, housing_lmy], ignore_index=True)

# Step 5: Clean the data by removing rows with missing values and infinite values
housing_df = housing_df.replace([float('inf'), -float('inf')], pd.NA).dropna()

# Step 6: Filter to 2021
housing_2021 = housing_df[housing_df['Year'] == 2021]

# Step 7: Split housing_2021 by income group
high_income = housing_2021[housing_2021['Income_Group'] == 'High Income']
low_income = housing_2021[housing_2021['Income_Group'] == 'Low/Mid Income']

# Step 8: Compute regression lines for both groups
slope_hi, intercept_hi, _, _, _ = linregress(high_income['GDP_per_capita'], high_income['Housing_Score'])
slope_lo, intercept_lo, _, _, _ = linregress(low_income['GDP_per_capita'], low_income['Housing_Score'])

# Step 9: Plot observations for each housing scores and corresponding GDP per capita, colored by income group
plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6))
plt.scatter(high_income['GDP_per_capita'], high_income['Housing_Score'], color='blue', alpha=0.6, label='High Income (2021)')
plt.scatter(low_income['GDP_per_capita'], low_income['Housing_Score'], color='orange', alpha=0.6, label='Low/Mid Income (2021)')

# Plot regression lines for each group
x_hi = high_income['GDP_per_capita']
x_lo = low_income['GDP_per_capita']
plt.plot(x_hi, slope_hi * x_hi + intercept_hi, color='blue', linewidth=2)
plt.plot(x_lo, slope_lo * x_lo + intercept_lo, color='orange', linewidth=2)

# Final formatting with labels, title, and legend
plt.xlabel("GDP per Capita (most recent year available)")
plt.ylabel("Right to Housing Score (Income Adjusted)")
plt.title("GDP per Capita vs. Right to Housing (2021, by Income Group)")
plt.legend()
plt.grid(True)
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()
  • Here, we can see that the relationship between GDP per capita and housing scores is negative for high-income and positive for low/mid-income.
  • However, the slope of the regression line is steeper for high-income countries, indicating a stronger relationship between GDP and housing scores.
  • In contrast, low/mid-income countries show a weaker relationship, suggesting that GDP may not be as strong a predictor of housing scores.
  • To further analyse data significance and quantify the relationship, we compute the folowing regression statistics.

#Step 1: we compute the slope, intercept, r-value, p-value, and standard error for both high-income and low/mid-income countries.
slope_hi, intercept_hi, r_value_hi, p_value_hi, std_err_hi = linregress(
    high_income['GDP_per_capita'], high_income['Housing_Score']
)
slope_lo, intercept_lo, r_value_lo, p_value_lo, std_err_lo = linregress(
    low_income['GDP_per_capita'], low_income['Housing_Score']
)

#Step 2: Print results
print("High-Income Countries:")
print(f"  Slope: {slope_hi}")
print(f"  Intercept: {intercept_hi}")
print(f"  R-value: {r_value_hi}")
print(f"  P-value: {p_value_hi}")
print(f"  Std. Error: {std_err_hi}")

print("\nLow/Mid-Income Countries:")
print(f"  Slope: {slope_lo}")
print(f"  Intercept: {intercept_lo}")
print(f"  R-value: {r_value_lo}")
print(f"  P-value: {p_value_lo}")
print(f"  Std. Error: {std_err_lo}")

  • The p-value indicates the statistical significance of the relationship between GDP and housing scores.

  • The p-value for high-income countries is 0.49, and for low/mid-income countries, it is 7.35E-12.

  • Using a threshold of 0.05, we can conclude that the relationship between GDP and housing scores is statistically significant for low/mid-income countries but not for high-income countries.

  • Therefore, while high-income countries show a negative relationship between GDP and housing scores, the relationship is not statistically significant.

  • In contrast, low/mid-income countries show a positive relationship between GDP and housing scores that is statistically significant.

  • This suggests that as GDP increases, housing scores also increase in low/mid-income countries.

Subsection 3: The relationship between educational rights and GDP

  • Introduction:

Education is a fundamental human right and a key driver of social and economic development. However, the quality and accessibility of education can vary significantly across countries, often influenced by economic factors such as GDP per capita. In this analysis, we aim to explore the relationship between a country's wealth and its ability to provide educational rights. Specifically, we seek to answer the question: "Do wealthier countries provide significantly better educational rights?"

  • Question: Do wealthier countries provide significantly better educational rights?

By examining education scores and GDP per capita across countries, we hope to uncover whether wealthier nations consistently outperform lower-income countries in providing quality education. This analysis will also highlight disparities between income groups and regions, offering insights into how economic resources impact educational outcomes. Ultimately, the findings may inform policy recommendations for improving educational rights in less affluent nations.
